The pulp is the living core of the tooth: vascular, innervated, defensive and formative. Through it, the tooth senses injury, protects the dentin–pulp complex, forms dentin in response to its environment and, in immature teeth, continues root development.

Endodontic treatment has made it possible to preserve teeth after pulpal disease. Infection can be controlled, the canal can be sealed and the tooth can remain in function for years. Pulp vitality remains the biological frontier.

Dental pulp regeneration extends that ambition toward vitality. It seeks to create the conditions for living tissue to return inside the root, allowing the tooth to recover biological capacities linked to defense, repair and continued dentin formation.

This research programme approaches that aim through three connected fronts: bioactive materials that shape the pulp’s environment, regeneration strategies that rebuild living tissue inside the canal, and innovations that expand what can be generated, predicted and designed.

02  /  The programme

A programme in three movements.

A living pulp requires the coordination of material behaviour, tissue formation and technological innovation.

Material

The material is the first biological encounter. It protects the wound, shapes the local chemistry and creates an environment the pulp can respond to.

Regeneration

Regeneration is the biological aim. The canal becomes a site where vascularized, dentin-forming tissue can organize across the real geometry of the root.

Innovation

Innovation changes what becomes possible. Cell reprogramming offers renewable routes toward odontoblastic-like cells. AI-driven design allows calcium-silicate cements to be imagined through target behaviours: setting, strength, alkalinity, ion release and biological response considered together.

Together, these lines of work frame pulp regeneration as a problem of design: shaping material behaviour, guiding tissue formation, and developing the innovations that make both more deliberate.

03  /  Pillar I · Matter for the living pulp

From filling to fulfilling.

A pulp-capping material is a surface, a chemistry and a signal that living tissue must interpret.

The pulp-capping interface — the material’s signal (amber) meets the pulp’s response (rose) as a new dentin bridge forms.

A material placed against the pulp occupies the space where healing must begin. It protects the exposure, but it also becomes the immediate environment in which pulp cells must survive, respond and move toward dentin formation.

For the clinician, the material must mix, set, seal and remain stable. For the pulp, it must shape a local environment that supports repair: a surface cells can meet, a chemistry they can tolerate, and signals that can guide mineralized tissue formation.

This is the central shift: from judging pulp-capping materials mainly by how they handle and harden, to designing them by how they behave biologically after placement. Chemistry, alkalinity, structure and surface influence what the tissue senses, how it reacts and whether repair can become dentin.

The material occupies a clinical space, but its deeper role is biological. It is placed where the tissue has been injured, and its purpose is to help that tissue heal with direction.

The material is where clinical protection begins to shape biological repair.
04  /  Pillar II · Regenerating pulp as tissue

A living canal.

The root canal becomes a place where pulp vitality can be rebuilt.

The root canal repopulated — living, vascularized tissue flowing from the apex (right) toward the chamber (left), lined by a forming odontoblast layer.

Regeneration is the formation of organized tissue. Vessels, cells and matrix must connect along the root as a viable pulp-like structure, capable of remaining alive and contributing to dentin formation.

Tissue formation has to occur within a narrow and elongated anatomy, where survival depends on vascularization, organization and integration with the surrounding dentin.

The root canal becomes more than the site of treatment. It becomes the anatomical space where pulp vitality can be reconstructed.

05  /  Pillar III · From intuition to design

Innovation makes regeneration more deliberate.

Regeneration depends on choices too numerous for intuition alone.

From materials to design to tissue — biomaterials feed a computational design network (amber) whose signal becomes the living pulp (rose) inside the crown.

One side is cellular. Cell reprogramming creates a renewable route toward odontoblastic-like cells, expanding the biological sources available for dentin-pulp regeneration and making cell fate part of the design of the therapy.

The other side is material. AI-driven design allows calcium-silicate cements to be imagined through target behaviours: setting, strength, alkalinity, ion release and biological response considered together, before a formulation is treated as final.

Together, these innovations change the scale of the problem. Regeneration is no longer guided only by what can be tested one step at a time. It becomes a design space where cells, materials and biological responses can be generated, predicted, prescribed and validated.

Method gives that space a direction.

06  /  Pillar IV · Reading the field

The models shape the questions.

A field advances by learning how to read its own results.

The same biology read through different models — one lens faithful, one distorted, one partial.

Models and assays shape what the field is able to learn. A cell model can simplify the tissue it represents. A biological assay can be influenced by the material being tested. A result can appear meaningful until the model behind it is examined more closely.

This part of the work asks how pulp regeneration should be studied: which models suit which questions, which comparisons are fair, which readouts are informative and which artifacts can mislead interpretation.

A field matures when it learns how to understand what its measurements can mean.
07  /  In view

The programme in view.

Matter, tissue, innovation — one ambition.

Dental pulp regeneration sits at the intersection of matter, tissue and innovation.

Materials define the first biological encounter. Regeneration gives the canal a living aim. Innovation expands what can be generated, predicted and designed. Reading the field keeps the work connected to the models and meanings through which the science advances.

Together, these lines form a research programme around one clinical and biological ambition: restoring the conditions for pulp vitality inside the treated tooth.
