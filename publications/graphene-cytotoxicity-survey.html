What makes commercial ‘graphene’ toxic isn’t the graphene

Thirty-six commercial products sold as “graphene” ranged from harmless to highly toxic. The cause was not graphene itself but the contaminants left behind — so every product must be checked.

The problem

‘Graphene’ is a label, not a material

Graphene is moving fast into composites, electronics, membranes, drug delivery and implantable materials — but the supply has outrun quality control. Many products sold as "graphene" are poorly characterized black powders, and the published evidence on their safety is openly contradictory.

Part of the reason is that "graphene" names a whole family of materials, not a single substance. Across 36 commercial products, carbon content alone ranged from 63 to 98 wt%, alongside wide variation in flake size, number of layers, structural defects and contaminants — and more than a third were highly defective graphene.

With suppliers' safety datasheets often missing or inadequate, a buyer has little way to know what is actually in the bottle — or whether it is safe for cells.

The core difficulty

“Across 36 products sold as ‘graphene’, cytotoxicity ranged from completely harmless to highly toxic — with no shared specification.”

The approach

How the toxicity was measured

Every product was tested as received, with no extra processing that could skew the result, on a standard fibroblast cell line following the ISO 10993-5 cytotoxicity protocol. In parallel, each material was characterized in depth — Raman and XPS for structure and defects, elemental analysis and thermogravimetry for composition and contaminants.

Toxicity was read two complementary ways. LDH release reports plasma-membrane rupture — the % cytotoxicity — while WST-1 reports mitochondrial activity: viable cells cleave the reagent into coloured formazan, so less colour means lower viability.

Dose mattered too. Some products that looked cytocompatible at low concentration became highly toxic at higher ones — a reminder that a single test condition cannot settle a material's safety.

The finding

Same graphene, opposite outcome

The decisive result came from comparing look-alikes. Two products with nearly identical graphene — the same high carbon content, the same low level of volatiles, the same intact structure — gave very different toxicity. Two others, both highly defective with the same low carbon content, also split: one harmless, one toxic. No single graphene property could explain it (Fig. 3 and Fig. 4).

What did track toxicity was contamination. The fraction of persistent inorganic impurities rose with cytotoxicity, and washing the products to strip surface impurities cut their cytotoxicity by at least 30% in three of four toxic samples. The harm came from the contaminants — residues of the raw graphite and the synthesis — not from the graphene.

The cause
Impurities, not graphene
Persistent inorganic and organic contaminants — left by the raw graphite and synthesis — determined cytotoxicity; removing them made the products markedly safer.
Why it matters

From black box to benchmark

For anyone using commercial graphene, the practical message is that a product label is not a specification. "Toxic" and "non-toxic" are not fixed properties of "graphene"; they depend on what each batch actually contains and on how it will be used.

Until shared standards and honest safety datasheets exist, the burden falls on the buyer: independently characterize structure, purity and toxicity before use, and consider a purification step for commercial material. Quality control and proper characterization are what make graphene safe to develop with.

What's next

Harmonized testing protocols, quality standards for commercial graphene, and routine purification and characterization before research or product use.
