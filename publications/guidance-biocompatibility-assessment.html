Why biocompatibility depends on how you test it

Biocompatibility is not a fixed property of a material but a conclusion shaped by the cells, exposure route, dose and assay chosen. This guidance shows how to make that conclusion trustworthy.

Key takeaways

Customize the test to the intended use and exposure route — standards are a starting point, not a strict prescription.

Match the cells to the target tissue; the right model is critical to a meaningful result.

Guard against artifacts with the right controls and reference materials, to separate true toxicity from interference.

Report every step transparently — preparation, execution and reporting all shape a material's biocompatibility.

The problem

The same material, different conclusions

Biocompatibility is often treated as a fixed label — a material is simply "safe" or "toxic." In practice, the same biomaterial can pass in one study and fail in another. Outcomes depend heavily on the experimental setup: the cell type used, how the material is presented, the dose tested, and how long cells are exposed.

Standards such as ISO 7405 and ISO 10993 bring valuable structure, letting laboratories compare data worldwide and screen materials against recognized thresholds. But they cannot anticipate every clinical scenario, and some endorsed cell lines — derived from mouse connective tissue (L929) or hamster lung (V-79) — are not obviously relevant to a material placed in the mouth.

The result is a literature that can look contradictory. Making sense of it requires tests built around how a material is actually used — its intended application, the tissue it contacts, and the route by which it reaches living cells.

The core difficulty

“The same biomaterial can read as safe or toxic depending on the cells, dose and assay the test uses.”

The approach

Match the test to the intended use

A meaningful test starts from the material's intended application and the way patients are exposed to it. The exposure route — direct contact with pulp tissue, diffusion through dentin, or release into saliva — decides whether to use a direct-contact assay or an eluate. It can even reverse the result: a cement may look harmless through a dentin barrier yet prove cytotoxic in direct contact.

Cell choice matters just as much, because cellular function varies by tissue. Endodontic materials are best tested on pulp-derived cells such as DPSC or SHED; periodontal materials on gingival fibroblasts, PDL stem cells or cementoblasts; bone-contacting materials on osteoblasts or the MG-63 line. Cell passage and culture time can shift responses, so they must be reported.

Specimen size, dose and contact time complete the design. Smaller cement discs released less and read as less toxic than larger ones, and brief exposures can miss effects that emerge only over time — so a dose-response range, rather than a single condition, gives the fairest picture.

The pitfalls

When the assay misleads

Many cytotoxicity assays report a proxy — a colour change or a fluorescent signal — that the material itself can distort. A reagent can react with the biomaterial and quench the readout, producing a false negative that hides real harm. Conversely, an alkaline calcium-silicate cement can raise the medium's pH and shift a colorimetric dye, producing a false positive while the cells are in fact still alive (Fig. 3 and Fig. 4).

The defence is built into the design. Include the right controls — material plus reagent, no cells — characterize autofluorescence and pH effects, and pair a colorimetric or fluorescence assay with a direct cell-count or membrane-integrity test. Reference materials, with similar chemistry and a documented safety history, give the measured values context.

The fix
Controls + reference materials
Pairing assays and including the right controls separates true cytotoxicity from material interference — the difference between a real result and an artifact.
Why it matters

From conflicting data to trustworthy conclusions

Biocompatibility is less a fixed property than a character that emerges from every choice in the test — cell, specimen, exposure, assay and reporting. Treating those choices deliberately, rather than by default, is what makes a "safe" conclusion actually mean something for the patient.

Transparent reporting closes the loop. Documenting the cells and their passage, specimen and medium preparation, controls, reference materials, any protocol changes and the statistics lets others reproduce and compare results — turning a scattered literature into evidence that regulators, clinicians and patients can rely on.

What's next

Emerging models — 3D organotypic cultures, ex vivo tooth slices and organ-on-a-chip systems such as tooth-on-a-chip — promise more physiologically relevant biocompatibility testing.
