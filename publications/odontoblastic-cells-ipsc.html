Odontoblasts, rebuilt from a patient's own pulp cells

Dental-pulp cells, rewound into induced pluripotent stem cells, become functional odontoblast-like cells that form tubular dentin in vivo — and keep their mineralizing power after long-term expansion, when ordinary pulp cells lose theirs.

The problem

Pulp cells run out — and run down

Odontoblasts are the cells that build dentin, and the best human source for studying them has been dental pulp stem cells (DPSCs). But primary pulp cells are scarce, donor-dependent, and — critically — they lose their differentiation and mineralization capacity the longer they are expanded in the lab.

That decline is a real bottleneck. Research on dentin–pulp regeneration, and any assay that uses these cells to test whether a biomaterial is bioactive, needs a supply of human odontoblasts that is both plentiful and stable over time.

Induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs) offer a way out: reprogrammed from a patient's own cells, they self-renew indefinitely and can, in principle, be guided into any cell type. The open question was whether iPSCs could be turned into genuinely functional odontoblasts — and whether they would hold onto that ability after long-term expansion.

The core question

“Could a patient's own cells be reprogrammed into a limitless, stable source of functional human odontoblasts?”

How it was done

Rewind to a stem cell, rebuild an odontoblast

DPSCs were reprogrammed into iPSCs by delivering five factors — OCT-4, SOX2, KLF4, L-MYC and LIN28 — by electroporation, an episomal approach that leaves no integrated transgene behind.

The resulting iPSCs were checked against the standard bar for pluripotency: they overexpressed the reprogramming genes, showed high levels of alkaline phosphatase, OCT4 and TRA-1-60, and — implanted in vivo — generated tissues from all three germ layers, while keeping a normal karyotype at early and late passage.

To steer them toward dentin-forming cells, the iPSCs were exposed to BMP-4 across a dose range; 100 ng/mL gave the strongest odontogenic response, switching on the odontoblast markers DSPP, DMP-1 and MEPE.

The functional test was in vivo: iPSC-derived cells were seeded on human dentin discs fitted with poly-L-lactic acid (PLLA) scaffolds and implanted. After 28 days they had formed a pulp-like tissue, with odontoblast-like cells lining newly deposited tubular dentin against the disc.

The aim was not just cells that look right, but cells that do an odontoblast's job — lay down organised, tubular dentin in living tissue.

The finding

They kept their power when pulp cells lost theirs

The decisive comparison was long-term expansion. iPSC-derived cells and DPSCs were taken to passage 4 and passage 14, then driven to differentiate with odontogenic medium or bioactive-cement extract.

Regardless of passage, the iPSC-derived cells expressed odontoblastic markers and deposited the same abundant alizarin-red mineral. The DPSCs at P14 could not: their mineralization dropped sharply and most odontoblast genes failed to rise. The iPSC route delivered a source that stays functional over time — exactly what a research and testing model needs.

The result
Stable to P14
iPSC-derived cells kept full mineralization and odontoblast markers at late passage; DPSCs lost most of theirs.
Why it matters

A renewable, stable source of human odontoblasts

By turning a patient's pulp cells into iPSCs and then into functional odontoblasts, the study removes the supply-and-stability ceiling that primary pulp cells impose. The cells can be expanded at scale without losing what makes them useful.

That opens a dependable human platform for studying dentinogenesis and dentin–pulp regeneration — and a consistent, physiologically relevant assay for testing whether new biomaterials are genuinely bioactive.

What's next

A scalable, patient-specific human odontoblast model — for regeneration research and for screening the bioactivity of pulp-capping and restorative materials.
